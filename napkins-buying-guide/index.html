How to choose a napkins

Updated June 2026Independently researchedNo paid placement.

Quick answer

Choose cloth napkins based on fabric, size, and quantity that match your lifestyle – linen for elegance, cotton for easy care. Napkin rings add a finished look but aren't essential; buy them after you settle on napkins. Aim for at least one set per person plus extras for guests.

What fabric should you look for in a cloth napkin?

The fabric determines how your napkins look, feel, and hold up over time. Linen is the classic choice for formal dining – it’s absorbent, gets softer with washing, and develops a beautiful natural crinkle. The tradeoff is that linen wrinkles easily and requires careful ironing or steaming. Many owners find that a light spritz of water and a hot iron is enough, but it’s not a set-it-and-forget-it fabric. Cotton is the workhorse of cloth napkins. It’s durable, machine-washable, and resistant to stains when pre-treated. Look for 100% cotton or a cotton-linen blend if you want some of linen’s texture without the high maintenance. Polyester blends are rare in nicer sets but appear in budget options – they resist wrinkles but can feel slick and don’t absorb as well. For everyday use, a midweight cotton percale or twill is a reliable choice. Performance fabrics like those with a stain-release finish are marketed for easy care. They’re often a cotton-polyester blend with a treatment that helps food stains lift in the wash. While not as luxurious as pure linen, they’re practical for families and frequent dinner parties. Read owner reviews to see how the finish holds up after many washes – some treatments can fade or yellow over time.

How many napkins does a household really need?

Start with the number of regular seats at your table, then double it if you want to avoid doing laundry midweek. A set of six or eight is common for a couple or small family; for larger households or frequent entertaining, a dozen is the sweet spot. Many brands sell napkins in sets of four, six, or twelve, but you can also buy individual napkins from some retailers to customise your count. Consider how often you entertain. If you host dinner parties for eight every month, a set of twelve lets you set the table with extras for spills. For daily use, plan on one napkin per person per meal rotation – meaning you need enough to last between laundry days. A good rule of thumb is four to six napkins per person if you wash weekly, though cloth napkin users often wash more frequently because stains set if left too long. Mixing sets is perfectly fine. Many stylish tables combine two or three complementary colours or patterns to create a collected look. Just make sure the sizes and fabric weights are similar – a heavy linen napkin next to a flimsy cotton one can feel unbalanced.

What size napkin is best for your table?

Cloth napkins come in three common sizes: cocktail (roughly 12 by 12 inches), luncheon or standard (about 18 by 18), and dinner (20 by 20 or larger). The dinner size is the most versatile – large enough to drape over your lap and tie a simple knot or fold into an elaborate shape. If you primarily serve plated dinners, go with dinner-size napkins. For casual weeknight meals, a standard 18-inch napkin is plenty and easier to handle. Luncheon napkins are ideal for brunch, tea, or smaller tables where a huge napkin feels overpowering. Cocktail napkins are really just for drinks and appetisers. Before you buy, check the table dimensions and whether you’ll fold the napkin flat or in a ring. A folded dinner napkin can take up significant table real estate, so if space is tight, consider sticking with standard size. Brands often list napkin dimensions in the product description. If you can’t find them, compare the set to a napkin you already own – the difference between an 18-inch and a 20-inch napkin is more noticeable than you’d think. When in doubt, buy one larger than you expect; you can always use a bigger napkin in a smaller setting, but a too-small napkin looks skimpy.

How should you care for cloth napkins?

Care starts with pre-washing. Most cotton and linen napkins shrink a little – up to 5 percent – so wash them before the first use in warm water and tumble dry on low. Check the care tag: linen often recommends a gentle cycle and line drying to preserve fibres. Cotton napkins can usually handle a regular wash, but high heat may set wrinkles. For daily use, a quick machine wash and a warm iron or steam press keeps them looking crisp. Stain treatment matters. Blot red wine or oil spills immediately with a clean cloth, then pre-treat with a dab of dish soap or a stain stick before washing. Avoid bleach – it weakens fibres and can yellow white napkins over time. For tough stains, soaking in an oxygen-based cleaner for an hour before washing works well. Many linen enthusiasts embrace minor stains as patina, but if you prefer pristine whites, separate napkins by colour and avoid washing with dark garments. Folding and storage affect how long napkins last. Store them flat or rolled rather than folded repeatedly along the same creases – those lines can become permanent, especially on linen. If you use napkin rings, remove them before washing; the metal or plastic can scratch or warp in the dryer.

Should you buy napkin rings?

Napkin rings are purely decorative but they add a polished finish to a place setting. They’re especially nice for formal dinners, holiday tables, or when you want to dress up a simple napkin. The ring holds the napkin in a clean roll or fold, and it can double as a placeholder – you can personalise rings or attach a name card. For everyday use, many people skip them because they add one more thing to wash and store. If you buy rings, consider the material and how it interacts with the napkin fabric. Smooth metal or wood won’t snag, while rough or painted finishes may catch on linen loops. Decide whether you want a traditional ring that slides over a folded napkin, or a more modern wrap style that fastens with a clip or magnet. The latter is easier to remove but can be bulkier. Storage is a practical concern. Rings need a drawer or a dish. Some sets come with a storage box, but many do not. If you entertain often and want a consistent look, buy enough rings for all your napkins. For occasional use, a set of six or eight rings is plenty – just match the style to your dinnerware or flatware finish.

How do you match napkins to the rest of your table setting?

Start with the tone you want: crisp white or neutral tones go with everything and make food pop. Coloured or patterned napkins can anchor a theme or add personality. If your dinnerware is busy, stick with solid napkins in a colour drawn from the plate pattern. If your plates are plain, you have more freedom – try stripes, plaids, or a subtle jacquard weave for texture. Napkin rings can tie the look together. A brass ring pairs well with warm-toned flatware; silver or chrome rings suit cooler palettes. For a rustic table, wooden or woven rings work nicely. Don’t over-coordinate – a little contrast looks curated, not matchy. For example, a white linen napkin with a dark blue ring and blue-striped plates feels intentional. Seasonality is a fun way to change things up without a full table investment. Light linens or cotton in spring and summer; deeper colours and richer textures like velvet or heavy linen in fall and winter. You can buy a single set of neutral napkins and swap rings seasonally to refresh the look affordably.

Frequently asked questions

Are linen napkins worth the extra cost?

Yes, if you value a luxurious feel, natural absorbency, and a fabric that softens over time. Linen costs more than cotton upfront and needs more care (ironing or steaming), but many owners say it lasts for years and looks better with age. For everyday use with children or heavy stains, cotton may be more practical.

Can I mix and match different napkin sets?

Absolutely. Mixing colours, patterns, or even fabrics is a popular way to create a relaxed, curated table. The key is to keep the sizes similar so they look intentional – a mix of 18-inch and 20-inch napkins can still work if you fold them the same way.

How often should I wash cloth napkins?

After each use if they have visible stains or food residue. For light use (e.g., just wiping fingers with a clean napkin), many people reuse the same napkin a couple of times before washing. Wash at least every three to four uses to prevent oils from setting.

What is the best way to fold cloth napkins for storage?

Fold them loosely along the original creases or roll them to avoid permanent lines. Stack flat in a drawer or basket; for linen, storing them in a breathable fabric bag helps maintain texture. Avoid hanging them – gravity can stretch the fabric over time.

Are napkin rings necessary for a nice table setting?

No, they are optional. Napkin rings add a decorative touch and can help keep a roll or fold secure, but a neatly folded napkin placed directly on the plate or to the left of the fork looks just as polished. Buy rings if you enjoy the detail; skip them if you prefer simplicity or have limited storage.

How do I remove stubborn stains like red wine from cloth napkins?

Blot the stain immediately with a clean cloth, then apply salt to absorb excess liquid. Pre-treat with a stain remover or a paste of baking soda and water, then launder in warm water with an oxygen bleach. Avoid hot water until the stain is gone, as heat can set the mark.

In shortUltimately, the best cloth napkin set is one you’ll actually use and care for consistently. Focus on fabric that matches your washing habits, buy enough to cover your table without daily laundry, and consider napkin rings as a finishing touch rather than a must-have. With a little planning, cloth napkins elevate everyday meals and make entertaining feel special – all while reducing paper waste.
