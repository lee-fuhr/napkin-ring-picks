Napkins questions, answered

Updated June 2026Independently researchedNo paid placement.

Quick answer

Cloth napkins add elegance and reduce waste, but choosing the right fabric, size, and care routine matters most. Napkin rings can dress them up or keep a set organized, though they’re not essential. Focus on 100% cotton or linen for absorbency and durability, and look for napkin rings that won’t snag the fabric.

What fabric is best for cloth napkins?

Linen is the traditional favorite for its natural texture, breathability, and ability to become softer with each wash. It’s highly absorbent and resists pilling, but it wrinkles easily and can be more expensive. Many hosts prefer linen for formal dinners precisely because of that relaxed, rumpled look. Cotton is the most practical everyday choice. A good-quality cotton napkin (like a percale or sateen weave) offers a nice hand feel and excellent absorbency without the high price tag of linen. Cotton-polyester blends are budget-friendly and resist wrinkles, but they don’t absorb as well and may feel less luxurious over time. For a compromise, some brands offer linen-cotton blends that balance durability and softness.

What size napkin should I buy?

Standard dinner napkins usually measure between 18 and 20 inches square. A 20-inch napkin is the most versatile: it folds neatly under utensils, covers the lap fully, and works for both casual dinners and more formal gatherings. Smaller 16-inch napkins are often sold as luncheon or cocktail napkins and are better suited for brunch or appetizer spreads. If you entertain frequently or enjoy elaborate folding, oversize 22-inch napkins give you more fabric to work with and create a dramatic table setting. For everyday use, a 20-inch cotton or linen napkin is the sweet spot – big enough to function well without overwhelming the table.

How do I care for cloth napkins to keep them looking good?

Wash cloth napkins before first use to soften them and remove any manufacturing residues. Most linen and cotton napkins can go in the washing machine on a warm or cool cycle with mild detergent. Avoid bleach, which weakens fibers over time – a scoop of oxygen bleach or a splash of white vinegar in the rinse cycle helps keep colors bright and removes odors. For crisper results, iron napkins while they are still slightly damp. Linen wrinkles quickly, so many people simply fold them damp and press with a hot iron. If you dislike ironing, take them out of the dryer while still a bit damp, smooth them flat, and let them air-dry. Napkin rings are best removed before washing to avoid scratches or tangles.

How to choose napkin rings that won’t damage fabric

Napkin rings come in wood, metal, acrylic, ceramic, and fabric-covered styles. Metal rings with smooth edges are fine for most fabrics, but rings with sharp seams, rough finishes, or intricate cutouts can snag or pull threads, especially on delicate linen. Stick to rings with a polished interior or a coating that won’t catch on weaves. Size matters too: rings that are too tight will crease the napkin permanently, while too-loose rings slide off. A typical ring diameter of 1.5 to 2 inches accommodates most folded napkins. Acrylic and resin rings are lightweight and gentle on fibers; wooden or ceramic rings add weight and visual heft but should be checked for splinters or rough spots before use.

How many napkins should I buy?

For daily use, plan on 12 to 16 napkins per person – that gives you a week’s worth of napkins before laundry day, with a few extras for spills or guests. If you entertain often, a set of 24 to 36 napkins allows you to set a table for eight to twelve guests while still having backups. Buying multiples of the same color and fabric makes mixing and matching easy, and you can rotate them so wear is even. Many online shops sell napkins in sets of 4 or 6, so you can gradually build a collection without a big upfront cost. Avoid buying just one set of a very specific pattern; plain whites, creams, or subtle earth tones remain versatile and easier to replace later.

What are common mistakes to avoid when buying cloth napkins?

One frequent mistake is choosing a fabric that sheds lint heavily, like some cheap cotton weaves. Low-quality napkins can leave fuzzy bits on your clothing and look dingy after just a few washes. Another misstep is buying napkins that are too thin – they won’t absorb spills well and may feel flimsy on the lap. People also overlook shrinkage. Unwashed napkins labeled “pre-shrunk” can still shrink a few percent, so buy a size slightly larger than you think you need, or wash and dry them on hot once before measuring for your table. Finally, don’t ignore the napkin rings: buying rings that match your napkins only in color but not in feel can result in rings that bang against the table or scratch the fabric.

Frequently asked questions

Can I use cloth napkins for everyday meals?

Absolutely. Cotton or cotton-blend napkins are durable enough for daily use and save money and waste over time. Just keep a few extra sets so you always have clean ones on hand, and toss them in the laundry with your regular towels.

How often should I wash cloth napkins?

Wash them after every meal if they’ve been used for something messy or after one or two uses if they’re only lightly used. Rotating a large stash lets you go longer between washes without feeling the need to re-use a stained napkin.

Are linen napkins better than cotton?

Linen is more absorbent, gets softer over time, and has a natural elegance that many hosts prefer. Cotton is easier to care for, less prone to wrinkles, and generally more affordable. The “better” choice depends on your budget, your table style, and how much ironing you’re willing to do.

What’s the best way to fold cloth napkins for a dinner party?

For a polished look, a simple rectangle or triangle fold is elegant and doesn’t require practice. If you want a classic pocket fold (for silverware), practice with a square napkin by folding it in half twice, then folding the top layer down diagonally. There are dozens of online tutorials for fancier folds, but simple folds always look intentional.

Do napkin rings damage the fabric?

Rings with rough edges, tight diameters, or sharp clasps can snag woven threads, especially on linen. To prevent damage, choose rings with a smooth interior finish, slide them on gently, and remove them before washing. Fabric-covered or padded rings are the safest for delicate napkins.

Can I mix and match napkin sets?

Absolutely, and it’s one of the easiest ways to personalize a table. Mix solid-color napkins with a subtle patterned one, or use different but complementary shades on each place setting. The key is keeping the fabric weight roughly the same so folds and drapes remain consistent.

In shortThe right cloth napkins and rings can transform a meal from routine to special without breaking the bank or creating extra hassle. Start with a versatile set of medium-weight cotton or linen in a neutral shade, pick rings that feel smooth and secure, and build your collection over time. With simple care, they’ll last for years and make every dinner feel a little more polished.
